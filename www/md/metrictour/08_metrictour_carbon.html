Climate adaptation planning addresses the impacts of climate change via efforts to increase landscape resilience and other strategies. However, it is important to place climate adaptation strategies within a larger context which includes mitigation strategies to address the causes of climate change by reducing accumulation of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere. An example of such a dual adaptation/mitigation strategy is a recent conservation proposal termed the Global Deal for Nature. The Global Deal for Nature proposes that protected area networks be expanded to include areas that hold large reserves of aboveground (biomass) and belowground (soil and biomass) carbon, with the aim of reducing disturbances that accelerate release of stored carbon (Dinerstein et al.Â 2019). The sliding view in the map to the right allows comparison of the distribution of aboveground tree carbon (left hand panel) and soil carbon (right hand panel). In North America, soil carbon is generally at highest levels in large boreal peatlands, implying that protection of large boreal landscapes is critical for conserving areas of high soil carbon. Patterns of above-ground carbon are complex and vary based on several broad-scale climatic and other factors. A strong longitudinal precipitation gradient is evident in North America, as predominantly west-to-east atmospheric circulation interacts with north-south cordilleras such as the Rocky Mountains. More mesic forested ecosystems to the west of these ranges generally support higher levels of aboveground carbon in terms of tree biomass. Although grassland ecosystems can also support high levels of belowground carbon in their root mass, we have not yet located comprehensive spatial data representing carbon levels for North American grassland systems.
